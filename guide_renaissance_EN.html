Introduction

This guide
is for you

Perhaps you woke up this morning feeling that a part of you has never truly been yours. Perhaps you have felt pain where there should have been pleasure, numbness where you longed to feel, shame instead of intimacy. Perhaps you have felt alone in all of this — because this subject is not spoken of, not told, often not understood even by those you love most.

If you have experienced female genital mutilation (FGM), your body underwent something you did not choose. You are not responsible for what happened. And you are not alone: worldwide, over 200 million women share this experience.

This guide was created to offer you something concrete: a gradual, respectful, and scientifically grounded path to reclaim sensitivity, reduce pain, and rediscover pleasure — without surgery, without judgment, at your own pace.

What you will find in this guide

A four-phase programme, designed to be experienced week by week:

  • How your body works and why sensitivity can be retrained
  • Breathing and relaxation exercises for the pelvic floor
  • Pain desensitisation techniques and sensory re-education
  • Practices to reconnect with pleasure safely and autonomously
  • Guidance for communication with a partner (if and when you are ready)
  • Resources to find specialised professionals
⟡ Always remember

Chapter 1

Understanding your body:
what remains, what is possible

Many women believe that after FGM, there is nothing left to feel. This belief is understandable — but it is neurologically inaccurate. Here is why.

The clitoris: so much more than meets the eye

The visible clitoris (the external part) is only the "tip of the iceberg". The total clitoral structure extends 9–11 cm inside the body, with two roots (crura) and two bulbs that surround the vaginal canal. Even in cases of FGM Type III (the most extensive), much of this structure remains intact and functional.

Neuroplasticity: the brain can relearn

The nervous system is extraordinarily adaptable. When a part of the body stops sending signals — through trauma, scarring, or disuse — the brain "redraws" those connections. The good news is that this process also works in reverse: through graduated stimulation, conscious breathing and muscle relaxation, it is possible to awaken nerve pathways that had "fallen asleep".

✦ Scientific evidence

Clinical studies conducted in France and the United Kingdom show that over 70% of women who followed a pelvic sensory re-education programme reported significant improvement in sensitivity and reduction in pain within 3–6 months.

Phase 1: Breathing and Pelvic Relaxation

Before any work on the body, we must create safety. The nervous system needs to learn that relaxation is possible, that the body can be a safe place.

🌬️
Diaphragmatic Breathing
⏱ 10 minutes · twice daily

  • Lie on your back, knees bent, feet flat on the floor. Place one hand on your abdomen and one on your chest.
  • Inhale slowly through the nose for 4 seconds: feel the abdomen rise upward, the chest remains almost still.
  • Hold the breath for 4 seconds, keeping the body soft and relaxed.
  • Exhale through the mouth for 6 seconds, as if blowing towards a distant candle.
  • During the exhale, imagine the pelvic floor "melting" downward, like wax softening.
  • Repeat for 10 minutes. If emotions arise, let them pass without judgement.
⚠ Important note

In this phase, do NOT touch the genital area. The goal is only awareness and relaxation of the nervous system. Give your body time to feel safe.

2
Weeks 3–4

Phase 2: Body Awareness and Mapping

In this phase we begin to "rewrite the map" of your body. The goal is to know — without judgement — the landscape of sensations that exists now.

🤲
Neutral Touch Exercise
⏱ 15–20 minutes · every 2 days
  • Prepare your environment: soft light, comfortable temperature, privacy assured. You may add relaxing music.
  • After 5 minutes of diaphragmatic breathing, begin to explore your body with your hands: arms, abdomen, inner thighs.
  • Use different types of touch: light pressure, moderate pressure, circular movements, gentle stroking. Note what you feel — or do not feel.
  • Gradually approach the external perineal area. If you feel discomfort, return to the thighs and continue another day.
  • Do not seek pleasure in this phase — you are simply taking inventory of present sensations.
3
Weeks 5–8

Phase 3: Sensory Re-education and Desensitisation

This is the most delicate and transformative phase. We will work on desensitising painful areas and progressively stimulating pleasurable sensitivity.

🎯
Gradual Desensitisation
⏱ 20 minutes · 3 times per week
  • Before the exercise, take 5 minutes of diaphragmatic breathing to activate the parasympathetic nervous system.
  • Begin by touching neutral zones (thighs, lower abdomen) for 2–3 minutes.
  • With a slightly oiled finger, touch the outer edge of the scar tissue. If you feel sharp pain, stop. If you feel only mild tension, stay there with slow breathing for 30–60 seconds.
  • The "therapeutic window" is the zone between no discomfort and intense pain. This is where re-education happens.
  • After each session, give the body 10 minutes of recovery breathing. Do not underestimate this step.
4
Weeks 9–12 and beyond

Phase 4: Integration and Reclaiming Pleasure

In this final phase we explore pleasure as a right, not as a goal to achieve. Every pleasurable sensation you find belongs to you — and to no one else.

✨
Autonomous Pleasure Exploration
⏱ 20–30 minutes · when you are ready
  • Create an environment of total safety and privacy. Bring something you enjoy: music, a scent, a soft blanket.
  • Begin with breathing (5 minutes) then a body scan to "arrive" in your body.
  • Explore the entire body with curious touch — you are not seeking genital pleasure, you are discovering where your body responds with enjoyment: the backs of the knees? The nape of the neck? Shoulders?
  • Gradually bring attention to the pelvic area using techniques learned in previous phases. If pleasure emerges, welcome it without expectations.
  • Remember: orgasm is not the goal. Pleasure itself — even the smallest moment of non-pain — is an enormous victory.
Professional Support

You do not have to do
this alone

🏥 Pelvic Physiotherapy

A physiotherapist specialised in the pelvic floor can assess muscular hypertonia and guide you with personalised manual techniques.

🧠 Somatic Psychotherapy

Approaches such as EMDR, Somatic Experiencing or Sensorimotor Therapy work on bodily trauma safely and progressively.

👩‍⚕️ Specialist Gynaecologist

To assess the specific condition of scars, possible stenosis, and discuss medical options including reconstructive surgery if desired.

💬 Support Groups

FORWARD UK, Daughters of Eve, Orchid Project, and Desert Flower Foundation offer resources and community for women affected by FGM.

✦ UK Resources

The NHS FGM Health Passport programme and specialist FGM clinics exist across the UK (including UCLH in London, St Mary's Manchester). The NSPCC FGM helpline: 0800 028 3550.

✦ International Resources

WHO/UNFPA Joint Programme to Eliminate FGM, UNICEF, and Equality Now provide multilingual resources and connections to trained professionals worldwide.
